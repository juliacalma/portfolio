Artificial Intelligence — Case Study (COMP717)

I explored how game theory shows up in AI by running/analysing three number-guessing games (V100, V10A, V10B), then writing a short report on AI connections (multi-agent systems, self-play, GANs, and mechanism design).

Problem

Use simple games to study rational behaviour and equilibrium ideas, then explain how those ideas transfer to AI settings. Games: V100 (pick 1–100, target is 2/3 of average), V10A (same, with prior round info), and V10B (1–10, winner closest to 1.5 × average of two players).

Data & Tools

Data: class submissions (numbers + short reasons) across the three games; my compiled classification table (H/M/I).
Outputs: a concise AI report (PDF) and the classification table (PDF).

Methods

  • Defined a lightweight rubric for player types: Highly Rational, Moderately Rational, Irrational/Random (keywords like “2/3”, “average”, “1.5×”, vs “random”, “birthday”).
  • Tagged entries and noted shifts in reasoning across V100 → V10A → V10B.
  • Explained Nash equilibrium for each game and why actual results differ from the ideal.
  • Connected findings to AI: multi-agent reasoning, self-play, adversarial setups (GANs), and mechanism tweaks.

My Role

  • Compiled and cleaned the raw responses.
  • Built the H/M/I classification table and checked consistency.
  • Wrote the AI report (2 pages) linking the games to AI practice.

Results (highlights)

  • V100 (first exposure): many “lucky/favourite” picks; limited strategic depth → wins looked mostly random.
  • V10A (with prior info): more “2/3 of last answer” reasoning → moderate shift toward rational adaptation.
  • V10B (1.5× average, 1–10): most winners chose 10; behaviour aligned with the mechanism and the equilibrium.

Learnings

  • Mechanisms shape behaviour: small rule changes (2/3 × avg vs 1.5 × avg) push players toward very different strategies.
  • Depth of reasoning matters: level-k/common-knowledge effects explain gaps between theory (equilibrium) and what people do.
  • Clear rubrics help: simple keyword-based tagging (H/M/I) makes classroom data easy to analyse at scale.
  • AI link: the same ideas drive multi-agent AI, self-play, GANs, and auction design—agents adapt to the rules and to each other.
